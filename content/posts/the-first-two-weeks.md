# The First Two Weeks

Asana gives everyone a sabbatical at four years. I joined knowing that and I'd been looking forward to mine. I also knew what unstructured time could do, having previously taken a [five-month break between jobs](https://hiremaga.com/posts/letting-go-finding-flow) a few years ago, the first long pause of my career.

The first day was a Saturday. It didn't quite feel like the first day yet, but was the perfect start. Varun's baseball team won their finals. I rode my motorcycle to the field and stayed for the whole game, watching him field and bat and celebrate with his teammates. That evening I went to a mindful sketching workshop at the Dharma Collective on 24th Street. I've been journaling more since, and I discovered [Lynda Barry's](https://www.drawnandquarterly.com/author/lynda-barry) approach there. Her books remind me I want to draw more, the way I used to when I was Varun's age.

Then Monday came, and my calendar was just empty. I kept looking for something urgent to do. I was planning the whole sabbatical in the first three days. By evening, as I got more tired, I found it harder to remember I was on my break.

Getting in the pool helped my restless melt into calm.

Swimming starts my day. I wrote that down in my journal when it finally felt true. After a swim the rest of the day feels like mine. Bonnie Tsui puts it well in [*On Muscle*](https://bonnietsui.com/on-muscle), a book I've been reading: "Exercise is a way to grapple with existence, to act." I read [a piece by Maria Popova](https://www.themarginalian.org/2026/05/24/roger-deakin-waterlog/) that described swimming as subversive, "a way to break free of the official version of things." I wrote in the margin that this felt like learning Rust right now.

---

I've also been learning Rust again, starting with [Rustlings](https://github.com/rust-lang/rustlings), the first step in a curriculum I've designed for myself. I set a bar for myself: can I explain it, not just compile it. After each section [I write down what I actually learned](https://github.com/hiremaga/rustlings/commit/c51d80bf9c02ada00b79f3fae0eecd2679872f38). If I can't describe why a derive macro works after the exercise, I'll probably forget what I've learned much sooner. Taking the time to learn something deeply like this calms me emotionally. In the past it has added up to knowledge I've been able to share and apply for years. I'm not late to the party, I'm merely arriving when I'm actually ready.

I'm still working out what AI means for me as a programmer. For now I'm using it to understand more, not to know less. For Rustlings, I've asked Claude to be [a pedagogially skillful tutor](https://github.com/hiremaga/rustlings/blob/main/CLAUDE.md). This has worked well so far, it's challenging me to focus, dig deeper than I might on my own, and helped me cross hurdles that might've otherwise become overwhelming.

Matthew Crawford makes a related point in [*Shop Class as Soulcraft*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/304793/shop-class-as-soulcraft-by-matthew-b-crawford/) that's been on my mind: "The disappearance of tools from our common education is the first step toward a wider ignorance of the world we inhabit." He was writing about trades. He could have been writing about AI in 2026.

---

Last Saturday I rode my motorcycle to Tomales Bay with friends. Highway 1, the bay appearing suddenly around a bend. We stopped for excellent pizza at [Route One Bakery](https://www.routeonebakery.com). I've been riding for about a year and a half. Every trip still surprises me a little, that I can just go somewhere on my own and find my way back.

I've had a few long conversations with people I hadn't really talked to in too long. When you're not saving your energy for a roadmap review, you actually show up to talk.

One Tuesday, Madhavi and I went to Golden Gate Park with a book about California birds, naming what flew past. A dog found me at Crissy Field and wanted to play. It was the most wonderful thing. I've been writing these things down. I don't want to forget them.

---

There have been hard days too. On one of them I wrote, at 8:37 in the morning: "This, whatever this is, is really hard. I'm sad so much of the time. Scared. Lonely. Disoriented." I was sitting in the Bay Club locker room in my swim trunks, about to get in the pool.

I kept coming back to the same things anyway. Swim. Ride. Sit with Varun while he tells me about something I don't fully understand yet. I finished Simone Stolzoff's [*How to Not Know*](https://simonestolzoff.com) walking from the pool to Columbus Avenue one Thursday morning. He writes about finding the things you keep returning to, and holding onto them. Swimming, riding, writing. That's been true for me.

---

Rustlings is nearly done. Next comes building small tools, then [*Rust for Rustaceans*](https://nostarch.com/rust-rustaceans). I'm looking forward to getting my hands on something real.

In just over a week I fly to Bangalore. My grandmother passed away in March. I haven't been back since. There are others too, people I won't get to see again. And people I can't wait to hold.

I'll write more from there.

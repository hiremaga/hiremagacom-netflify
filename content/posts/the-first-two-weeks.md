---
title: The First Two Weeks
date: 2026-05-31
---

Asana gives everyone a sabbatical at four years. I'd been looking forward to mine for a long time. I knew what unstructured time could do. I'd taken a five-month break between jobs a few years ago, the first long pause of my career, and it had changed me.

Knowing all that didn't make the first week easy.

The first day, Varun's baseball team won their finals. I was there for the whole game, in the sun, watching him field and bat and celebrate with his teammates. That evening I went to a mindful sketching workshop on 24th Street. It was a great way to kick things off. I've been journaling more since, and I'm excited to learn Lynda Barry's approach and to doodle and draw the way I used to when I was Varun's age.

Then the quiet came.

The calendar empties and you find out how much of your sense of self was on loan from your schedule. I kept reaching for urgency that wasn't there. I caught myself planning too much, filling time before I'd learned to be in it.

What helped was simple. Get in the pool.

Swimming has anchored most mornings. I wrote that down when I finally felt it settle in, not as an intention but as a fact. The water asks nothing except that you show up. It doesn't know what quarter it is. After a swim, the rest of the day feels chosen rather than fallen into. Bonnie Tsui puts it plainly in *On Muscle*, a book I've been reading. "Exercise is a way to grapple with existence, to act." That's what it is.

-----

I've been learning Rust, starting with Rustlings, the first step in a curriculum I've designed for myself. The bar I set is simple. Can I explain it, not just compile it. After each section I write down what I actually learned, not as a log but as a test. If I can't articulate why the borrow checker works the way it does, I haven't learned it yet.

I'm still working out what AI means for me as a programmer. For now the answer is to use it to understand more, not to know less. I've set up Claude as a tutor with a strict rule. Earn the answer through your own attempt first, hints before solutions, explanation before moving on.

I read a piece by Maria Popova this week that described swimming as subversive, "a way to break free of the official version of things." I wrote in the margin that this felt like learning Rust right now. Crawford makes a similar point in *Shop Class as Soulcraft*, which I have from the library. "The disappearance of tools from our common education is the first step toward a wider ignorance of the world we inhabit." He was writing about trades. He could have been writing about now.

-----

Last Saturday I rode to Tomales Bay with a friend and my cousin. Highway 1, the bay appearing suddenly around a bend. We stopped for excellent pizza at Route One Bakery. Riding is still new enough that every trip feels like proof of something, that I can go where I want, on my own terms, and find my way back.

I've had a few long conversations with people I hadn't really talked to in too long. When you're not managing your energy for a roadmap review, you actually show up to talk.

On a Tuesday, Madhavi and I went to Golden Gate Park with a book about California birds, naming what flew past. Yesterday I played catch with a friendly Aussiedoodle who wandered over at Crissy Field. Small moments that are the content of a good life.

-----

There have also been hard moments. Disorientation is real. Some mornings started sad and restless and unmoored, with scarcity thinking that had no obvious reason to be there.

What I kept coming back to was simple in hindsight, almost embarrassingly so. Show up anyway. Swim. Ride. Soak in the time with Varun while he tells me something about Minecraft or DnD that I can't fully understand yet. I read Simone Stolzoff's *How to Not Know* at a North Beach cafe and sat with it after. His idea is that the things you keep returning to are anchors. That word felt right.

Swimming. Learning. Reading. Riding. Sitting with Varun. I keep coming back to the same things. This feels right.

-----

Rustlings is nearly done. Next in my progression comes Tokio, building small tools, and *Rust for Rustaceans* in the background. I'm looking forward to putting together something more whole.

The, in just over a week, I fly to Bangalore. My grandmother passed away on March 2nd. I haven't been back since. There are others too, people I won't get to see again after my last visit in 2024. And others still, people I can't wait to hug once I get there.

I'll write some more then.
